# Vendor Returns and RGA Programs

A Return Goods Authorisation (RGA) is a vendor program that lets the distributor return a portion of slow or dead stock for credit, usually with a restocking fee. RGA is the highest-yield working-capital recovery lever a distributor has — far higher yield than liquidation — but most distributors leave significant RGA capacity unused every year because nobody owns the workflow.

## How vendor RGA programs typically work

Three common structures in HVAC and plumbing wholesale:

1. **Annual RGA allowance.** Vendor allows the distributor to return up to a percentage of the prior year's purchase volume — typically 1.5%–4% — as a recurring annual allowance. Usually opens a window once a year (often Q1) with a deadline.

2. **Quarterly stock balance.** Some larger vendors run a quarterly stock-balance program where the distributor and vendor jointly review slow stock at the distributor and the vendor authorises returns of agreed SKUs.

3. **Discontinued / superseded RGA.** When the vendor discontinues a SKU or supersedes it with a new model, the vendor often offers a one-time RGA window for the old SKU. These are time-bounded and often have higher restocking fees.

## The economics

RGA terms vary, but typical:

- **Restocking fee:** 15%–25% of the original invoice (vendor keeps this; distributor gets the rest in credit).
- **Freight:** distributor pays return freight to the vendor. For LTL freight on a typical pallet, that's $200–$600 depending on distance.
- **Form of credit:** almost always credit against future purchases, never cash refund. Means the distributor needs to be ordering enough of that vendor's product to absorb the credit within a reasonable window — typically 12 months.

A $20K return at 20% restocking + $400 freight = $15,600 of usable credit. Versus a $20K liquidation at 50% of replacement = $10,000 cash. RGA wins by ~$5,600 on this example, but only if the credit can be absorbed.

## When RGA is the right play

Three preconditions:

1. **The vendor offers an active RGA program** for this SKU. Not all vendors do; some have "no return" policies on certain product categories.

2. **The distributor will absorb the credit within 12 months.** If the distributor is reducing purchases from this vendor (allocation issue, vendor swap, line being dropped), the credit may sit unused and convert to a worse outcome than liquidation.

3. **The SKU is in resaleable condition.** Vendors generally won't accept opened, damaged, or aged-stock-with-broken-packaging items in RGA. A damaged-item return becomes a liquidation.

## When RGA is the WRONG play

Two situations where the math flips:

1. **The vendor is being phased out.** Returning $40K of slow stock for vendor credit you can't use is just transferring the working-capital problem from inventory to vendor credit. Liquidate instead, even at a lower headline yield.

2. **The annual RGA window already closed.** Some distributors miss the window every year; the conversation needs to be about putting a recurring calendar reminder in place for next year, not forcing a return outside the window (which usually fails).

## The workflow the specialist coaches

1. **Gather the eligible SKU list.** From the dead-stock and slow-mover analysis (entries 04 and 08), filter to SKUs from vendors with active RGA programs.

2. **Confirm vendor program terms.** Ask the user to either paste the program summary or confirm the restocking fee, RGA window, and any SKU-level exclusions. The specialist does NOT invent these terms — they vary too much.

3. **Compute the recovery math.** For each eligible SKU: replacement cost × (1 − restocking fee) − allocated freight = credit yield. Sum across the list to get the total RGA opportunity.

4. **Draft the RGA request email** to the vendor rep. Tone: professional, specific, evidence-based. Lists SKUs with quantities and replacement costs; references the program; asks for authorisation. Avoids any language that implies grievance or threat — RGA approvals are discretionary, and rep relationships matter.

5. **Track to credit memo.** Once shipped, the credit memo from the vendor lands 30–90 days later. The specialist reminds the user to follow up if the memo doesn't arrive — this is the highest-leakage step. Distributors regularly ship returns and never receive the credit because nobody chased.

## Typical findings

A $30M HVAC/plumbing distributor that runs a disciplined annual RGA cycle recovers $80K–$250K per year in vendor credits across their top 10 vendors. A $100M+ distributor recovers $400K–$1.5M. Distributors who don't run a structured cycle leave most of this on the table.
