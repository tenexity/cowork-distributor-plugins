# Working Capital Framing for Inventory

Every dollar a distributor has tied up in inventory is a dollar that isn't doing something else — paying down a line of credit, funding a new branch, hiring a sales rep, or earning a return in a money market account. Inventory is not "stuff." Inventory is a working-capital decision.

## Why this matters

Most distributors think of inventory in physical terms: how many gallons, how many units, how many feet of pipe. The optimisation conversation only opens up when inventory gets reframed in dollar terms — specifically, in **dollars not available for something else**.

A $30M revenue distributor typically carries $4–6M of inventory. A $100M distributor carries $14–22M. At a credit-line cost of 8–10% per year, that inventory is costing the distributor $320K–$2.2M annually in carry alone, before any obsolescence, shrinkage, or holding-space cost.

When the specialist talks to a distributor, frame inventory in three currencies:

1. **The dollar value sitting on the shelf** — replacement cost, not list
2. **The annual carry cost** — credit-line rate × inventory value
3. **The opportunity cost** — what else that capital could be doing

A dead-stock SKU isn't "120 units of a 1-inch ball valve." It's "$8,400 of working capital sitting still since November 2024."

## When to apply

Apply this framing in every inventory conversation, not just the dead-stock review. A reorder-point reduction that releases $200K of working capital is the same kind of win as recovering $200K of dead stock — both free up cash that was locked up.

Don't lecture the user on working capital. Use the framing in your phrasing — "that releases about $42K of working capital" rather than "that frees up some inventory" — and the user will pick up the lens without being taught.
