# True Carrying Cost of Inventory

The carrying cost of inventory is the all-in annual cost of owning a dollar of stock for a year. Most distributors estimate it at "the credit line rate" and stop there. The real number is roughly **20–28% per year** for HVAC and plumbing wholesale distribution.

## The components

Five line items add up:

1. **Cost of capital** — credit-line rate or weighted average cost of capital. Typical: 7–10%.
2. **Storage cost** — warehouse space, racking, lighting, climate control if applicable, share of building cost. Typical: 4–8%.
3. **Handling cost** — labour to receive, put away, count, pick, and re-stock. Typical: 3–6%.
4. **Obsolescence and shrinkage** — the percentage of inventory that becomes unsalable each year (expired, damaged, superseded, miscounted, walked away). Typical: 3–6%.
5. **Insurance and tax** — property tax on inventory in some states, insurance premiums. Typical: 1–2%.

Adding these gets you to **20–28%** total annual carrying cost. A $100K dead-stock SKU is costing the distributor $20K–$28K per year just to keep on the shelf. That's the math that turns "we'll move it eventually" into "we should liquidate this at 60 cents on the dollar tomorrow and stop bleeding."

## When to apply

The full carrying cost matters most in two conversations:

1. **Liquidation decisions.** When deciding whether to discount dead stock by 30%, 50%, or 70% to clear it, the loss has to be compared to **carrying cost × years until likely sale**, not just the discount on the day of sale. Holding a $50K SKU for two years at 24% carrying cost burns $24K — the 50% discount that looked painful turns out to be the better economic choice.

2. **New SKU adds.** When sales asks "can we bring in this new line?", the conversation should include carrying-cost math on the projected first-year inventory investment. New lines that look profitable on margin alone often look very different once carrying cost is in the model.

## How to phrase it for the user

Use the percentage, then translate to dollars:

> "Holding $80K of dead stock at 24% all-in carrying cost is about $19K per year — roughly $1,600 a month — to keep it sitting there. Against that, a 40% discount to clear it costs $32K once. The 40% discount pays for itself inside two years on the carry alone."

That kind of phrasing turns an emotional decision (admitting a buy went wrong) into a financial one.
