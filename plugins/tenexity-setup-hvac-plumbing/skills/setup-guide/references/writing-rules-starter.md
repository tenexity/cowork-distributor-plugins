# Reference: Writing rules starter template

The user's workspace already contains a pre-filled writing-rules.md at `ABOUT ME/writing-rules.md`. Read that file for the full content.

## Structure

The file has two halves:

**Pre-filled half (don't change):** Banned generic-AI words and phrases, banned SaaS/consultant jargon (distributor-specific), structural patterns to avoid, formatting rules, a non-negotiable "no invented numbers" rule, and a five-point quality test. These are universal anti-AI and anti-corporate-speak patterns — keep them intact. They're what stop Claude sounding like a robot or a sales deck.

**Personal half (customise during onboarding):** The "Your voice" section at the bottom has:
- Tone — 3-5 words describing their style
- Spelling — American or British
- Things you always do — their writing habits
- Things you never do — what feels wrong to them
- Your natural phrases — how they actually talk
- Additional banned words — industry buzzwords to avoid beyond the universal list

## What makes a good "Your voice" section for a distributor

The difference between useful and useless writing rules is specificity.

**Weak tone:** "Professional but friendly"
**Strong tone:** "Direct, plain-spoken, trade-fluent. American English. I use part numbers in every email. Contractor-grade language — 'on the truck tomorrow' beats 'we will ensure expedited fulfillment.' Never salesy."

**Weak "things you never do":** "Don't be too formal"
**Strong "things you never do":** "Never start with 'I hope this finds you well.' Never sign off 'Best regards' (too formal for our trade — 'Thanks,' or first name alone). Never apologise more than once in a message. Never over-promise on lead time — I'd rather lose the order than carry the blame."

**Weak natural phrases:** "I'm pretty casual"
**Strong natural phrases:** "On the truck tomorrow." "Let me pull that." "Send me the PO, we're good." "That's a hard no from the vendor." "Let me come back to you with the numbers."

During onboarding, if the user gives vague answers about their voice, nudge with examples: "Think about how you'd text a contractor you've known 15 years with an update. What words do you actually use? What do you never use?"

## Distributor-specific banned words to suggest

The pre-filled "Additional banned words" section lists common distributor fluff to consider banning:

- "full-service distributor" (every distributor claims this — meaningless)
- "stocking distributor" (same — prove it with fill rate, don't claim it)
- "white glove"
- "premier partner"
- "trusted advisor"
- "one-stop shop"
- "value-add" / "value-added" (unless literally describing kit-building or pre-assembly)

Offer these during the writing-rules step. Most distributors will adopt most of them. Add any they're particularly allergic to.

## Why this matters

Writing rules are what stop Claude sounding like a robot. Without them, every user gets the same generic AI voice. With the anti-AI + anti-SaaS-jargon sections alone, output gets noticeably better. With the personal voice section filled in, output starts sounding like the actual distributor.

This is the file that makes the biggest immediate difference to output quality. Worth spending a few minutes on.
